Your Story Toolkit

A workbook to help you prepare to talk with employers about where you've been and where you're going.

You picked up this booklet, and that matters. What you write in here is yours — nobody is grading it, nobody is using it against you, and you can stop any time you want. The questions get easier the further you go, and the hard ones have a skip box right next to them. When you're done, you'll get a guide: a story draft, some job ideas, and a few interview answers to practice.
— Mike Thibideau
President & CEO, Invest Hamilton County

How this works

  1. Answer the questions at your own pace. Skip anything you don't want to answer.
  2. Your answers are saved on this device only — nothing is sent to a server.
  3. When you're done, you'll see a summary you can print or download.
  4. Most people finish in 30–60 minutes.
What this toolkit is NOT: It is not a test. It is not a legal document. It is not a medical record. It is not shared with anyone unless you choose to share it.
Section 2 · Consent & your rights

Before you start — please read

Voluntary participation

Filling out this toolkit is your choice. You can stop at any time and your answers stay with you.

What happens to your answers

This beta version runs entirely in your web browser. Your answers are saved only on this device (in local storage). Nothing is sent over the internet. You can delete everything with the "Start over" button.

One thing to know: Indiana law requires reporting of a few specific things — current child abuse, current elder abuse, or a clear plan to hurt yourself or someone else. In the full program with staff support, those things would be reported. In this self-serve beta, please do not write anything you wouldn't want shared. If you're in crisis, call or text 988.

What you get back

At the end, you'll see a summary of your answers. You'll be able to print it, download it as a file, or copy it to paste into another tool.

Section 4 · Skills

What you can do

Check anything you've done — even once, even if you weren't paid, even if it was a long time ago. Skills count whether you learned them on a job, at home, or somewhere else.

Q4.a Skills checklist (check all that apply)
Q4.b Three things people say I'm good at

Even small things count — "I'm patient with kids," "I can fix anything," "people trust me."

Section 5 · What happened

What happened

Employers who ask about your past usually just want to know two things: that you've thought about it, and what's different now. You don't have to explain everything. A few sentences is enough. If any question here feels like too much today, check Skip and move on.
Q5.a In one or two sentences, what happened?

Example: "I got a drug charge in 2019." That's enough.

Q5.b When was it? (year is fine)
Q5.c Is the case closed?
Q5.d Optional — only if you want to. One thing about why — no blame, just honest.

Example: "I was using. I'm not anymore." You can leave this blank.

Section 6 · What's different now

What's different now

This is the part employers listen to most. What you've done since — what you've learned, what you've changed — matters more than what happened before.

Q6.a Since then, I have: (check all that apply)
Q6.b One specific thing I'm proud of since then

Example: "I've been clean 14 months." "I finished my GED."

Q6.c What I've learned about myself

Example: "I do better with a routine." "I can stay calm when everyone else panics."

Section 7 · Recovery (optional)

Recovery

You do not have to share this with employers. Ever. People in recovery are protected under the Americans with Disabilities Act. If recovery is part of your story and you want it to be part of your plan, answer here. You can skip this entire page.
Q7.a Are you in recovery?
Q7.b How long?
Q7.c Do you need any of these at work?

MAT medications like methadone, buprenorphine (Suboxone), and naltrexone are legal prescriptions. Employers cannot fire you for taking them as prescribed. (EEOC Opioid Guidance, 2020)

Section 8 · The job you want

The job you want

Q8.a Job or type of work I want

Example: "CDL truck driver." "Welder." "Line cook." "Something outside."

Q8.b Why this one?

What makes it a good fit? Money? You're good at it? A friend does it?

Q8.c Anything that might stop me that I should know about?
Some jobs have legal rules about who can be hired — healthcare, childcare, driving (CDL), banking, schools. You're not expected to know the rules. Just write what you're worried about.
